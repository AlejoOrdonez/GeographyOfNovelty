## Abstract

Predicting ecological novelty (communities without historical counterparts) requires shifting the focus from drivers of change to the processes underlying community restructuring. I introduce a process-based framework centred on four community assembly processes — dispersal, abiotic filtering, biotic interactions, and ecological drift — as spatially mappable predictors of where, when, and how novel ecosystems emerge. This structured, bottom-up approach links large-scale biodiversity and environmental data to ecological theory. Each process can be mapped and forecasted using species occurrence, traits, and environmental data to analyse spatial patterns of process intensity. By adopting this approach, we can pinpoint areas of novelty, identify different pathways of emergence, and recognise regions with unique ecological constraints. Examples from global terrestrial ecosystems illustrate each component. Our framework complements existing biodiversity models by emphasising that novelty results from how assembly processes respond to disturbance and climate change across space and time. Understanding these processes enhances ecological forecasting and informs strategies for biodiversity conservation, restoration, and transformation. This insight is vital for managing rapid ecological changes in the Anthropocene, enabling more informed and proactive governance of future ecosystems.
